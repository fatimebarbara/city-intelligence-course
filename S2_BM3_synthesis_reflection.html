Reflection 01 — Reframing intelligence

Section 2 showed you why cities fail. Section 3 has shifted the question: what does a system need in order to succeed? In your own words, describe what city intelligence means — and why that definition matters for the work you do.

This is not a test of recall. It is an invitation to make the definition yours. The difference between knowing a concept and owning it is the ability to state it in your own language, with your own examples.

Reflection 02 — Your system's strongest layer

Looking at the four layers of the concept map — data and information, governance and institutions, decision and action, outcomes and impact — which layer is strongest in your current context? What makes it strong, and what does that strength depend on?

Intelligence does not require every layer to be equally strong. It requires the connections between layers to be functional. Understanding where your system is strongest is the starting point for understanding which connections are most at risk.

Reflection 03 — The weakest connection

Intelligence breaks down at the transitions between layers — not usually inside them. Which transition in your system is currently the most broken? Where does something that exists in one layer fail to reach the next one?

This is the most practically useful question in the section. The four break points — data that isn't interpreted, interpretation that doesn't become action, action without outcome measurement, outcomes that don't feed back into data — are not equally common. Identifying yours precisely is the first step to addressing it.

Reflection 04 — The condition you most need to build

Of the four conditions — circulation, feedback, alignment, time horizon — which one is most absent from your context right now? Not which is most important in the abstract: which is most limiting in your specific situation?

The four conditions interact — a weakness in one tends to amplify weaknesses in the others. But they don't all need to be built simultaneously. Identifying the most limiting condition is the basis for strategic sequencing: which one, if strengthened, would most unlock the others?

Reflection 05 — One structural move

Based on your reflections above: name one structural change — not a communication improvement, not a new report, not a meeting — but a structural change that would genuinely strengthen the weakest condition or the most broken connection in your system. What would it change, and who would need to support it?

The distinction between structural change and surface improvement matters. Producing a better report is not a structural move. Redesigning where reports go and who has a mandate to act on them is structural. This question is asking for the harder, more important answer.
