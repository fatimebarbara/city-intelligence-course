Synthesising your learning from Section 5

Section 5 asked you to stop learning about the CII and start using it. These five reflections consolidate that shift — and prepare you for Section 6, where the diagnosis becomes an intervention design.

These prompts are harder than in previous sections — because the work is harder. Section 5 asked for a real diagnosis, not a conceptual exercise. The reflections here ask you to be honest about what the diagnosis revealed and what it changed.

Reflection 01 — What the diagnosis revealed

What did your diagnosis of your city or policy area reveal that you did not already know — or did not have a precise language for? What does the CII framework make visible that your existing analytical tools were not showing you?

The diagnostic template and the 20-minute exercise are designed to surface structural patterns that ordinary institutional knowledge doesn't easily name. If the diagnosis confirmed everything you already knew, the framework may not yet be working as a lens. If it revealed something new or clarified something previously vague, name it precisely.

Reflection 02 — Health as invisible infrastructure

The first video of this section argued that health is the accountability layer — the outcome that reflects the accumulated impact of every other domain. In your city or research context, which non-health decision has the most significant health consequence that is currently invisible to the people making that decision?

This is the most important conceptual contribution of Section 5. Health is not a sector — it is a result. The diagnostic power of this insight only activates when you can name a specific decision in a specific domain and trace its health consequence. A general agreement that "everything affects health" is not yet a diagnosis.

Reflection 03 — The weak signal you are watching

Name one weak signal that is currently visible in your city or field — something that is measurable now but not yet a headline problem. What does the CII framework suggest it is signalling about structural performance? And what is the risk if it is ignored for another 5 years?

Weak signal reading is a practitioner skill, not just a conceptual one. It requires connecting a small, early data point to a structural interpretation of where the system is heading. This reflection asks you to do that — specifically and concretely — rather than describe the concept in the abstract.

Reflection 04 — The attribution question you cannot yet answer

Your diagnosis produced an attribution question — which decisions are most likely producing the pattern you see. What additional evidence, expertise, or research would you need to answer that question with confidence? What is currently stopping you from answering it?

The CII generates attribution questions — it does not answer them. Knowing precisely what you would need to answer your attribution question is the beginning of an action plan, a research agenda, or a commissioning brief. This reflection asks you to translate the diagnosis into a specific evidence gap.

Reflection 05 — From diagnosis to intervention

Section 6 asks you to design an intervention based on your diagnosis. Before you get there: based on what your diagnosis revealed, what type of intervention does your city most need — not in terms of policy area, but in terms of structural type? A connection that needs to be built? A feedback loop that needs to be closed? A decision that needs to be reframed? A time horizon that needs to be extended?

The structural type of intervention is more important than the policy domain. Two cities can have identical health outcomes and require entirely different interventions — one needs coordination architecture, the other needs a feedback mechanism, the third needs a political reframing of what counts as success. Your diagnosis should be pointing you toward the structural type before you design the specific policy response.

Before the Section 5 quiz — a final self-check

Section 6 — Designing interventions

You have a diagnosis. You have an attribution question. You have identified the structural type of intervention your city needs. Section 6 asks: what makes an intervention intelligent — and how do you design one that addresses the structural gap your diagnosis has revealed? The quality of your Section 6 work depends entirely on the quality of the diagnosis you bring to it. The more specific your attribution question, the more useful the intervention design framework will be.
