Reading your city's trajectory

The CII measures structural performance at a point in time. This tool teaches you to read change over time — how to interpret score movements, which signals to watch first, what trajectory confirms your strategy is working, and what would tell you to adjust.

A city with a Foundational score of 0.55 that was 0.42 eighteen months ago is in a fundamentally different situation than a city with a score of 0.55 that was 0.68 eighteen months ago. The score is identical. The trajectory is opposite. This tool is about reading the trajectory — not just the position.

Not all CII pillar movements tell you the same thing at the same time. Some pillars move first — they are leading signals that predict what Healthy Society will do next. Others move later — they confirm what structural investments have already produced. Knowing which is which determines what you watch first, and how long you wait before concluding that a strategy is or is not working.

Leading signals

Foundational and Integrator — they move first

Watch these to know what is coming

Foundational and Integrator are leading pillars. They measure structural capacity — the conditions that make everything else possible. When these move upward, Healthy Society outcomes typically follow 12–24 months later. When these stagnate or decline, Healthy Society deterioration is predictable even before it appears in the data.

Why this matters for monitoring: If your strategy is investing in Foundational conditions (digital access, educational attainment, institutional trust), do not expect Healthy Society scores to move in the first year. The leading signal is the Foundational movement. The lagging confirmation is Healthy Society improvement. Expecting the lagging signal first will produce a premature conclusion that the strategy is not working.
Lagging signals

Healthy Society — it moves last, but it matters most

Watch this to confirm the strategy is landing

Healthy Society is the course's accountability layer — the question of whether structural investments are translating into actual wellbeing for residents. It moves last because it reflects the accumulated effect of structural capacity improvements. A strategy that produces strong Foundational and Integrator movement but no Healthy Society movement after three years has a translation problem — not a data problem.

Why this matters for monitoring: Healthy Society movement confirms your strategy. Foundational and Integrator movement predicts it. If you are twelve months in and Foundational has moved but Healthy Society has not — that is expected. If you are thirty-six months in and Healthy Society has not moved despite strong Foundational gains — that is the diagnostic signal that the translation mechanism is broken, and the attribution question from Section 5 needs to be asked again.
Watch carefully

Innovator, Regenerative, Connected — context-dependent

Depends which pillar your sequence prioritised

These three pillars are neither universally leading nor universally lagging — their role in your monitoring framework depends on where they sit in your compounding sequence. If Innovator was your Step 2 intervention, it is a leading signal for the Connected or Integrator gains that follow. If Regenerative was your Step 3, it is itself the lagging confirmation of the structural investments in Steps 1 and 2.

Why this matters for monitoring: Read these pillars relative to the sequence you designed — not in isolation. A flat Innovator score in Year 1 may be entirely expected if Innovator was your Step 2 and Step 1 (Foundational) is still building. A flat Innovator score in Year 2 — when Step 1 has moved — is the signal that the handoff between steps is not happening.

The monitoring cadence is not a reporting schedule — it is a structural decision about when to read, what to read, and what the reading means for the strategy. Four design questions determine the right cadence for your specific context.

How often should you measure?

Annual measurement is the minimum for a compounding strategy. Less frequent measurement means you may miss early-warning signals before they become strategy-threatening. More frequent measurement (quarterly) risks over-responding to noise.

Annual for Healthy Society and Foundational. Biannual for Integrator (governance capacity can shift faster). Quarterly for the disaggregated distributional signals the CII cannot provide — those require supplementary monitoring.

Which pillar do you read first?

Always read Foundational and Integrator before Healthy Society. They are the leading signals. Reading Healthy Society first creates the temptation to interpret the lagging signal as evidence of strategy failure when the leading signals are still moving.

Review order: (1) Foundational, (2) Integrator, (3) your sequence-specific pillar (Step 2 of your compounding sequence), (4) Healthy Society. Only draw conclusions after reading all four in sequence.

What is the adjustment threshold?

Define in advance what signal would trigger a sequence adjustment — before you start measuring, not after. An adjustment threshold defined after the fact is a rationalisation, not a governance decision.

Example threshold: "If Foundational has moved +0.05 or more and Healthy Society has not moved after 36 months, we trigger an attribution review." The number is less important than the pre-commitment.

Who reviews, and what authority do they have?

The monitoring review must be connected to the shared accountability framework from Section 9 — the body or role that owns the sequence as a whole. A review conducted by a single department will produce department-level conclusions, not strategy-level ones.

The shared accountability framework owner conducts the review. The review produces one of three conclusions: continue, adjust, or escalate. Each conclusion must be recorded and the rationale preserved for transition-proofing.

What the CII cannot show about trajectory

Structural limit

The CII shows aggregate trajectory — not distributional change

A Healthy Society score that rises from 0.35 to 0.42 over two years is real progress. But that aggregate movement tells you nothing about whether the improvement reached the populations who most needed it. The aggregate can improve while the distributional gap between the city's best-served and worst-served populations widens — if the improvement is concentrated in already-strong areas.

What this means for your monitoring framework: The CII trajectory gives you the structural signal. You need supplementary data — district-level, population-stratified — to give you the distributional story. These are two different monitoring tracks. Both belong in your framework. The CII tells you whether the average is moving. The disaggregated data tells you whether the gap is narrowing.
Structural limit

Score movement is not evidence of causal impact

A Foundational score that rises after your intervention is consistent with your intervention having caused it. It is not evidence that your intervention caused it. Other factors — national policy changes, demographic shifts, economic cycles — also affect pillar scores. The CII tracks correlation in structural performance, not causal attribution.

What this means for your monitoring framework: The monitoring framework tracks whether the strategy is consistent with the expected trajectory — not whether it caused it. For causal claims, a research design (comparison cities, pre-post analysis, difference-in-differences) is required. The CII provides the outcome measure. The research design provides the attribution.
