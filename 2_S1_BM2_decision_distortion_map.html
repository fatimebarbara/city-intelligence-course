How failure modes distort decisions

Select a failure mode to see how it distorts specific types of urban and policy decisions — and what you can do about it. Use this before entering a meeting, reviewing a policy, or assessing a data system.

Structural failure mode

Fragmentation

Data exists across departments, sectors, and systems that don't communicate. The city is measured in silos — and decisions are made as if each silo is the whole picture.

How it distorts each decision type

Resource allocation

Spending based on incomplete information

Each department bids for budget using its own data, creating duplication or competing claims that no one can reconcile at a systems level.

Warning: "We don't have that number"

Policy design

Solutions that solve one problem, create another

A housing policy designed without mobility data — the policy looks coherent within its domain, but breaks the system across domains.

Warning: "That's not our mandate"

Accountability

No one is responsible for the outcome

When systems are fragmented, the outcome of an intervention belongs to no single actor. Success and failure both become ungovernable.

Warning: "That falls between departments"

Crisis response

Slow coordination, conflicting data

In emergencies, fragmentation becomes visible: different agencies report different numbers, incompatible systems slow action, trust erodes between institutions.

Warning: "Our figures show something different"

Three counter-moves

Move 01

Map which datasets you need but don't have access to — before any decision, not after.

Move 02

Name at least one adjacent department whose data would change the recommendation.

Move 03

Ask: what would a connected view of this problem reveal that our silo cannot see?

Diagnostic question for your context

In the last major decision your institution made — what data did you use? Whose data wasn't in the room?

Cognitive failure mode

Over-measurement

Too many indicators, too many dashboards — but no clear prioritisation. Decision-makers are overwhelmed or anchored to whichever metric is most visible, not most meaningful.

How it distorts each decision type

Priority-setting

The urgent crowds out the important

When every indicator carries equal weight, attention flows to what's loudest or most recently measured — not what most affects long-term outcomes.

Warning: "We track 47 KPIs"

Communication

Too much data, no clear message

Reports that list every indicator available create the appearance of transparency without the substance of clarity. Signal cannot be extracted from noise.

Warning: "It's all in the appendix"

Evaluation

Success becomes whatever moved

With no pre-defined priority, success is retrospectively defined by whichever indicator improved — even if the most important ones remained flat.

Warning: "We saw improvement across several metrics"

Strategic planning

Plans that say everything, commit to nothing

Strategies built on exhaustive indicator lists lack the prioritisation logic that would make them actionable. Every goal is listed; none is ranked.

Warning: "All of this is important"

Three counter-moves

Move 01

Force a ranking: if you could track only 3 indicators this year, which would they be — and why?

Move 02

Link every indicator to a specific decision it enables. If it doesn't change a decision, question whether it needs to be tracked.

Move 03

Separate lagging indicators (what happened) from leading indicators (what is happening) — they serve different decision purposes.

Diagnostic question for your context

Which 3 indicators in your current reporting system most directly affect the decisions you make? Do your reports lead with those?

Institutional failure mode

Translation gap

Insights are produced — often at high quality — but never reach the people with a mandate to act on them. Evidence and implementation remain separate worlds.

How it distorts each decision type

Policy adoption

Evidence-based policy that ignores the evidence

Research exists, recommendations have been made — but the research never entered the decision-making room in a form the decision-maker could act on.

Warning: "There's a report on this somewhere"

Institutional learning

Repeated mistakes across administrations

When insights are not translated into institutional memory, lessons learned in one administration are lost in the next. The system re-discovers the same failures every cycle.

Warning: "We tried this before, but…"

Research commissioning

Studies designed for publication, not for use

When researchers and policymakers don't share a common question, the research answers the wrong thing — in the wrong language at the wrong time.

Warning: "The research came too late"

Cross-sector collaboration

Partners who report upward, not across

Data flows up to each institution's hierarchy but never across to partners who could use it. Collaboration is announced but not enabled by information architecture.

Warning: "We share our reports, but…"

Three counter-moves

Move 01

For every insight you produce, identify the specific decision-maker who could act on it — and the format that would reach them.

Move 02

Ask: if this finding arrived on a decision-maker's desk today, what would they need to act? Write that instead.

Move 03

Build a "decision-to-evidence" map: for each major upcoming decision, identify what evidence should be present — and whether it exists.

Diagnostic question for your context

Think of the last piece of research your team produced. Who received it? What did they do with it? Was that the intended outcome?

Political failure mode

Misaligned incentives

Decisions are shaped by political cycles, institutional constraints, or short-term pressures rather than the evidence of what produces long-term urban outcomes.

How it distorts each decision type

Investment decisions

Visible projects over systemic needs

Investment flows to what is visible or associated with a leader's legacy — rather than to the infrastructure gaps that most affect outcomes but attract least attention.

Warning: "This will transform our city's image"

Data collection

Measuring what flatters, not what informs

Institutions invest in measuring areas where performance is already strong, and underinvest in measuring the dimensions where gaps are real but politically inconvenient.

Warning: "We don't have reliable data on that"

Cross-mandate collaboration

Cooperation blocked by competitive accountability

When institutions are evaluated separately, cooperation requires each to give up the data or visibility that protects their own performance narrative.

Warning: "That would complicate our reporting"

Long-term planning

Plans designed for mandate, not for impact

Strategic plans are structured around the length of a political mandate — not the timescales at which urban systems actually change. The plan ends when the leader does.

Warning: "That's beyond our current mandate"

Three counter-moves

Move 01

Name the incentive structure explicitly: what is the institution currently rewarded for? How does that shape the decision on the table?

Move 02

Separate the decision from the announcement: what would the evidence recommend if no one were watching? Start there.

Move 03

Build a long-term cost case: what is the 10-year cost of not acting on the evidence? Make the incentive calculus explicit.

Diagnostic question for your context

What types of outcomes are most visible and rewarded in your institution? What types of outcomes are real but invisible to the incentive system?

Temporal failure mode

Short-termism

Systems react to immediate pressures instead of building long-term resilience. The most costly urban failures are often invisible in the short run — and catastrophic over time.

How it distorts each decision type

Infrastructure investment

Repair over prevention

Maintenance is chronically underfunded because its benefits are diffuse and future-dated. Crisis repairs, by contrast, are visible and generate political credit — so systems run to failure.

Warning: "We'll address that when it's a problem"

Public health

Treatment over prevention

Prevention generates savings over decades but struggles to attract investment because the outcome is a non-event: the disease that didn't happen.

Warning: "Prevention is hard to measure"

Climate adaptation

Mitigation that arrives too late

Urban climate decisions routinely discount future risk in favour of present cost, resulting in systematic underinvestment in adaptation.

Warning: "That's beyond our planning horizon"

Social cohesion

Fragmentation that builds slowly, breaks suddenly

Social trust erodes slowly and is rarely measured in real time. By the time the data shows the problem, the system has already been weakened for years.

Warning: "We didn't see this coming"

Three counter-moves

Move 01

For every major decision, ask: what is the 10-year and 25-year cost of inaction? Build this into the decision memo.

Move 02

Identify one leading indicator — something measurable today — that predicts the long-term outcome you care about. Start tracking it.

Move 03

Run a "pre-mortem": assume the city faces this problem in 15 years. What decision made today contributed to it? Act on that decision now.

Diagnostic question for your context

Which current policy is being shaped primarily by what's urgent this year? What would a 15-year lens reveal about that decision?
