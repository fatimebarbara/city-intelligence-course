Reflection 01 — The sequencing logic you arrived at

You have used the dependency mapper and the sequence design tool. Looking at the three-step sequence you built: is the starting point the structural priority — or the political priority? And if they are different, what does that gap tell you about the city or system you are working in?

The sequencing question is not purely analytical — it is political. The structurally correct starting point and the politically viable starting point are often different pillars. The most important diagnostic insight Section 7 can produce is naming that gap precisely: not pretending it doesn't exist, but not letting it permanently override the structural logic either.

PolicymakerIf the political starting point and the structural starting point are different, what would it take to make the structural starting point politically viable? Name a specific framing, stakeholder, or timeline shift.
ResearcherIf the political starting point consistently differs from the structural priority across cities, what does that pattern reveal about how urban governance systems select their intervention entry points?

Reflection 02 — The compounding test result

Apply the strictest version of the compounding test to your sequence: if you removed Step 1, would Steps 2 and 3 still work? If the answer is yes — even partially — your sequence is parallel, not compounding. What would need to change in the design to make the dependency genuine?

The compounding test is harder than it looks. It is easy to write a sequence that sounds like each step enables the next. It is much harder to design one where the dependency is real — where Step 2 is structurally blocked without Step 1, and Step 3 would produce a meaningfully different outcome if done after Steps 1 and 2 than if done alone. This reflection asks you to be honest about whether your sequence passes that test.

PolicymakerThe practical compounding test: if the administration changed after Step 1, and the new administration abandoned Steps 2 and 3, would Step 1 still have been worth doing on its own? If yes, that is a good sign — structural first moves should have standalone value even if the sequence is interrupted.
ResearcherThe analytical compounding test: what is the counterfactual for each step? What would the outcome be if Step 2 were done without Step 1 having preceded it? If the counterfactual is "roughly the same outcome," the compounding claim needs more precision.

Reflection 03 — The continuity problem

A compounding sequence requires continuity — in accountability, in time horizon, and in institutional memory — across all three steps. What is the most significant continuity risk in your sequence? Not the most likely external disruption, but the most structurally embedded threat to the chain.

The biggest threat to a compounding sequence is rarely external events. It is the institutional architecture of the system itself — the way governance structures fragment accountability, the way political cycles reset priorities, the way departmental mandates prevent anyone from being responsible for the sequence as a whole. This reflection asks you to name that structural threat specifically.

PolicymakerWho is currently accountable for the sequence as a whole — not just for one step? If no one is, that is the most important structural gap in your sequence design. Name who would need to be accountable and what authority that would require.
ResearcherWhich institutional feature of this city's governance is most likely to break the chain? Is it a mandate structure, an electoral cycle, a resource allocation process, or an accountability gap? Name the mechanism, not just the outcome.

Reflection 04 — The floor question

Section 7's central argument is that Foundational intelligence is the structural floor — that every other pillar's development is shaped by whether the conditions for participation exist. Does your city's Foundational performance set a ceiling on any of the other interventions in your sequence? If yes, is the ceiling manageable — or does it need to be raised before the sequence can fully work?

The floor question is the most important structural question in Section 7. A city can have an excellent intervention sequence targeting Innovator and Integrator pillars — and still see the sequence underperform because the Foundational floor is too low for the populations the sequence is meant to benefit. This is not a reason to delay everything until Foundational is perfect — it is a reason to design the sequence with the floor explicitly in mind.

PolicymakerWhich population groups are most likely to be excluded from the benefits of your sequence by a Foundational gap? Is there a way to design Step 1 so that it simultaneously closes a Foundational gap and creates the structural condition for the next intervention?
ResearcherDoes your research context have evidence on how Foundational performance constrains or enables upper-pillar development? What is the threshold at which Foundational adequacy stops being a binding constraint in the CII comparison group?

Reflection 05 — What Section 7 has changed in your thinking

Before Section 7, you had a diagnosis and an intervention design. After Section 7, you have a sequence. What has thinking about sequencing revealed that the diagnosis alone did not show? What question does the sequencing logic open that you did not have before?

This is the integration reflection — the one that tests whether the section has added genuine analytical value or just added a layer of complexity to what you already knew. The sequencing lens should reveal something the diagnosis didn't show: a dependency that wasn't visible, a starting point that was counterintuitive, or a compounding possibility that changes the scale of what's achievable. If it has not revealed anything new, the sequencing work may not yet be concrete enough.

PolicymakerWhat is the one thing your sequence suggests about your city's strategy that you could not have said before this section? Name the specific decision or conversation this changes.
ResearcherWhat research question does the sequencing logic open that your diagnostic work alone did not generate? Name the comparative hypothesis and the data you would need to test it.
