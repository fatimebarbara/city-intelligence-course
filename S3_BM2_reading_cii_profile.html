Section 4

Reading a CII profile: what the numbers reveal — and conceal

An index score is a signal, not an explanation. This material teaches you how to read a CII profile intelligently — including what to look for beyond the composite score, how to identify hidden tensions, and how to avoid the most common misreadings.

These profiles are illustrative — designed to demonstrate the analytical logic of the CII, not to represent any city's actual scores. In Section 5, you will work with real data. Here, focus on the method of reading, not the numbers themselves.

What the profile reveals

City A leads on innovation, connectivity, and foundational capacity. It has the infrastructure for a high-performing knowledge economy. A composite score of 0.74 would make it appear the strongest city in most traditional rankings.

The critical tension

A Healthy Society score of 0.44 — by far the lowest pillar — in a city that leads on innovation and connectivity signals a profound structural disconnect. Economic and knowledge performance is not translating into population health and wellbeing.

What the composite score hides

The composite of 0.74 is mathematically propped up by strong scores in five pillars. A traditional ranking would celebrate this city. The CII reveals that its residents are not living well — despite the headline performance.

The question this profile opens

Why is strong innovation and connectivity producing poor health outcomes? Is it inequality — strong performance concentrated in elite populations? Is it environmental — high innovation activity generating health externalities? The CII opens the question. It does not answer it.

The reading rule this profile teaches: Never read the composite score before reading the pillar profile. A high composite can mask a critical pillar failure. Always ask: which pillar is furthest from the average — and why?

The Healthy Society score of 0.38 is not just a low number — it is a structural warning. In a city where every other dimension is performing moderately well, a Healthy Society score this low suggests that urban outputs are systematically failing to translate into population wellbeing.

What the composite score hides

The apparent balance of City B's profile is misleading. Moderate performance across five pillars does not compensate for critically low Healthy Society performance. The question this city must answer is not "how do we improve on average?" but "why is our Healthy Society pillar structurally decoupled from the rest?"

City B's Foundational score of 0.63 suggests that the population has basic capabilities. But Healthy Society is critically low. This combination asks: are the health system, the urban environment, and the social infrastructure failing to convert foundational capabilities into actual health outcomes?

Its residents have good capabilities, live in a healthy environment, and receive coherent public services. For its population, this is a liveable, functioning city.

The critical tension

Low Innovator (0.41) and Connected (0.45) scores mean the city is not generating new knowledge or linking effectively to the world. Its current strengths — foundational, regenerative, integrator — are stable but not dynamic. The city is well-run but not positioned to shape its own future.

What the composite score hides

A composite of 0.58 places City C near the bottom of conventional rankings. A traditional index would rank it as underperforming. The CII reveals a more nuanced picture: for its residents' daily lives, it outperforms City A on the dimensions that matter most — health, environment, governance quality.

The question this profile opens

City C has the social and institutional foundations for innovation — but the knowledge engine hasn't been built yet. Is this a path-dependent gap (historical underinvestment in R&D) or a structural one (no university-industry linkage)? The CII identifies the gap; the policy question is whether it is closable and at what cost.

The reading rule this profile teaches: Composite rankings systematically undervalue cities that invest in people over performance metrics. City C's residents live better lives than City A's — but City A "wins" on the composite. Always ask: which composite are you reading, and for whom?

Five rules for reading any CII profile
1

Read the outlier pillar first, not the composite

The composite score averages across pillars and conceals the most important information. The pillar furthest from the average — either high or low — is where the city's most important structural story lives. Always start there.

2

Look for contradictions between connected pillars

Some pillars should move together: Foundational and Healthy Society, Innovator and Connected, Integrator and Regenerative. When they diverge sharply, something structural is interrupting the expected relationship. That interruption is a diagnostic signal worth investigating.

3

The score tells you where to look — not what you will find

A low Integrator score does not tell you whether the failure is in governance structure, administrative capacity, political will, or coordination architecture. It tells you the system is failing to coordinate. The explanation requires additional investigation.

4

Never use the CII to rank cities — use it to understand them

The CII is designed as a diagnostic instrument, not a competition. Using composite scores to rank cities misrepresents the tool's purpose and produces the same distortions as every other ranking system. The question is not "which city is best?" but "what does each city's profile reveal about its structural capacity?"

5

Ask whose experience the score reflects

A city-level composite can mask profound within-city inequality. A Healthy Society score of 0.70 may reflect average health outcomes that conceal a 10-year life expectancy gap between wealthy and peripheral districts. Always ask: who in the city does this score represent — and who does it not?

Common misreadings — and what to say instead

Misreading

"City X has a higher CII score, so it's a better city."

Accurate reading

"City X has a higher composite score. The pillar breakdown tells us which structural capacities are driving that score — and whether it is translating into residents' quality of life."

Misreading

"Our low Innovator score means we need to invest more in R&D."

Accurate reading

"Our low Innovator score signals that our knowledge creation and/or translation capacity is weak. Understanding which indicators drive the score tells us whether the problem is in generation, translation, or both."

Misreading

"Our Healthy Society score improved — our health policies are working."

Accurate reading

"Our Healthy Society score improved. Healthy Society outcomes are shaped by decisions in every domain — transport, housing, education, environmental policy. Attribution requires additional analysis beyond the CII score."

Misreading

"We score similarly to Copenhagen, so we face similar challenges."

Accurate reading

"Similar composite scores can reflect entirely different pillar profiles. The diagnostic value of the CII is in understanding how each city's structural configuration produces its outcomes — not in assuming similar scores mean similar situations."
