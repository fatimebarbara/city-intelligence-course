CM 01

Shared accountability framework

Countermeasure against: Mandate fragmentation

A formal structure in which two or more departments are jointly accountable for a shared outcome — with budget authority, reporting lines, and performance metrics that cross their individual mandates.

PolicymakerThe political challenge here is that shared accountability requires each department to accept partial credit for outcomes they cannot control alone. Name the departments and design the authority structure before the political resistance crystallises.
ResearcherFor research contexts: a shared accountability framework between research institutions and implementing bodies. Who holds the causal claim for the shared outcome — and who is accountable when the evidence doesn't translate?

Three design questions this countermeasure must answer

1. Who holds accountability for the sequence as a whole — and what budget authority do they have over the departments that own each step?

2. What happens structurally when a department's individual mandate conflicts with the shared outcome?

3. How is the shared outcome defined precisely enough that all departments know when they have collectively succeeded?

Name the departments, the accountability structure, the authority level, and the conflict resolution mechanism.

CM 02

Shared outcome metric system

Countermeasure against: Metric substitution

One metric — jointly produced, structurally visible, designed before the strategy launches — that no single department can satisfy independently and that all departments report against.

PolicymakerThe hardest design question is the metric itself. It must be jointly produced (no department can move it alone), structurally visible (in all departmental reporting), and designed before launch. A metric added after two years as an evaluation exercise does not count.
ResearcherThe research equivalent is the primary outcome in a multi-site trial: one agreed measure, tracked by all sites, that the intervention is designed to move. Designing it before data collection — not after — is the methodological non-negotiable.

Three properties the shared metric must have

1. Jointly produced: no single department can move it alone — it requires the combined effect of multiple steps.

2. Structurally visible: it appears in every department's reporting framework, not just the strategy owner's dashboard.

3. Designed at launch: agreed before the strategy begins, not commissioned as an evaluation after results are needed.

Name the metric precisely — not a category, not an indicator family. One measurable outcome that requires all departments in the sequence to contribute.

Name the individual departmental metrics most likely to substitute — the ones that would look like success while the shared outcome goes unmeasured.

CM 03

Transition-proofing protocol

Countermeasure against: Political cycle truncation

Three elements that embed the strategy logic in institutional architecture rather than individual memory: a strategy logic document, an embedded metric, and a cross-administration anchor.

PolicymakerThe transition-proofing protocol must be built into the strategy design before it launches — not added as a legacy document when you sense the political winds are changing. Its purpose is to make the compounding logic legible to an incoming administration that had no part in creating it.
ResearcherFor research contexts: the protocol is a pre-registration and data management plan that ensures the study can be completed and the data analysed even if the original research team changes. The causal chain must live in the protocol, not in the lead researcher's head.

Three elements of a transition-proofing protocol

1. Strategy logic document: a formal record of the causal chain — written so that an incoming leader with no prior knowledge can understand why interrupting the sequence would be costly.

2. Embedded metric: the shared outcome metric anchored in baseline institutional reporting, so it continues being tracked regardless of who is in office.

3. Cross-administration anchor: a formal commitment at the institutional level above the current administration that the sequence logic will be honoured through the transition.

Design all three elements specifically for your context. Name who holds what, where it lives institutionally, and what the anchor commitment looks like.

CM 04

Structural protection mechanism

Countermeasure against: Institutional capture

A formal separation between the authority to design the structural element and the authority to manage the programmatic element — so that the most powerful actor cannot redefine the structural mechanism without triggering an explicit governance review.

PolicymakerThe design principle is politically difficult: the department that implements the programme should not be the same entity that defines whether its structural mechanism remains intact. That definition authority needs an independent home — or it will migrate to whoever controls the budget.
ResearcherFor research contexts: the structural protection mechanism is a separation between the funding body's programme objectives and the research team's methodological integrity. The research design — particularly the primary outcome and causal mechanism — must be protected from funder-driven revision after the study has begun.

Two design questions this countermeasure must answer

1. Who defines whether the structural element of the intervention remains intact — and is that person or body independent from the implementing department?

2. What triggers a governance review if the structural mechanism is modified — and who has authority to restore it?

Name the structural element to be protected, who holds definition authority, and what the review trigger looks like.

Naming the most likely capture actor honestly — and acknowledging their legitimate interest — is the precondition for designing a mechanism they can accept.
