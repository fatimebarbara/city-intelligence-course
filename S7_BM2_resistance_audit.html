Section 7 · Resistance audit

Reading institutional resistance before it appears

For each of the four implementation failure modes: name the early-warning signal that would tell you it is beginning to operate in your context — before it has broken the chain. A governance countermeasure designed before the threat materialises is worth ten designed after.

The purpose of this audit is not to become pessimistic about implementation. It is to become specific. Vague institutional awareness does not produce governance countermeasures. Named signals do.

FM 01

Mandate fragmentation

Before the strategy launches: what would tell you that departmental accountability is already fragmenting the chain?

PolicymakerLook at how the departments in your sequence currently behave in joint working contexts. The signal is not hostility — it is disengagement. Departments that nod in shared meetings but never reference each other's work in their own reporting are showing early fragmentation.
ResearcherThe research signal for mandate fragmentation is citation asymmetry: do the departments' own reports and evaluations reference the other departments' work? If not, the institutional knowledge systems are already siloed regardless of what the formal governance structure says.

Not the full breakdown — the first observable signal that the chain is at risk. What would you see, hear, or read that would tell you this failure mode is beginning?

FM 02

Metric substitution

Before the strategy launches: what would tell you that departments are about to start reporting on their own metrics rather than the shared outcome?

PolicymakerThe most reliable early signal is the budget conversation. When departments begin describing their contribution to the strategy in terms of what they are spending rather than what outcome they are producing, metric substitution has already begun.
ResearcherThe research signal is in the evaluation design: who commissioned it, which indicators are being tracked, and whether the evaluation framework was set before or after the strategy was launched. Evaluations designed by individual departments almost always operationalise substituted metrics.

The moment when output measurement begins to replace outcome measurement — what does it look like in your specific institutional environment?

FM 03

Political cycle truncation

Before the strategy launches: what signals would tell you the sequence is not adequately transition-proofed?

PolicymakerThe clearest signal: can you describe the strategy to someone who has never heard of it in ten minutes, in a way that makes them understand why interrupting it mid-sequence would be costly? If you cannot, neither can the person who will need to make that case after you leave.
ResearcherThe research signal for truncation vulnerability is documentation quality. Can the strategy be reconstructed from its documentation by someone with no prior knowledge? If the logic lives only in the heads of the current team, the sequence will not survive the transition.

What happens to cross-boundary strategies in your context when political leadership transitions? What would tell you this strategy is not yet protected from that pattern?

FM 04

Institutional capture

Before the strategy launches: what would tell you that a powerful actor is beginning to reshape the structural elements of the intervention in their own interest?

PolicymakerThe earliest signal of capture is often found in staffing decisions and budget allocation requests: who is asking to manage the programme, and is their proposed governance structure subtly reassigning control of the structural elements to their domain?
ResearcherThe research signal for capture is in programme theory drift: compare the original intervention logic to the first operational plan. If the structural mechanism has been renamed, generalised, or repositioned as a "component" rather than the core, capture has already begun at the design stage.

Name the actor most likely to capture this intervention — and the specific mechanism through which they would do so. Not the hostile takeover — the gradual, well-intentioned redesign.
