Synthesising your learning from Section 4

Reflection 01 — The pillar that matters most to you

Of the six CII pillars, which one is most directly connected to the decisions you make or the research you conduct? Not which is most important in the abstract — which one is most immediately relevant to your current work?

This question is designed to personalise the framework before you use it diagnostically. The CII is most valuable not as a general framework but as a specific lens. Identifying your entry pillar — the one most relevant to your work — allows you to use the rest of the framework to ask better questions about the pillar you care most about.

Reflection 02 — The pillar you have been neglecting

Looking at the six pillars as a framework for your own decision-making or research: which pillar have you implicitly treated as less important — or have not considered at all? What does your neglect of this pillar reveal about the blind spots in your current framing?

Every policymaker and researcher has implicit pillar priorities shaped by their institutional mandate, disciplinary training, or political context. The CII is most useful not when it confirms what you already measure, but when it reveals what you have been systematically ignoring. This reflection asks you to name that blind spot honestly.

Reflection 03 — The composite score problem

This section introduced a critical methodological principle: composite indices reveal structural patterns that single indicators hide — but the composite score itself can conceal as much as it reveals. Describe a situation in your own context where an aggregate measure — a composite score, an average, a headline statistic — has been used in a way that hid a more important structural story. What was hidden, and what were the consequences?

This is not an abstract methodological question. Composite indices are used constantly in urban policy and research — and they are constantly misread. The ability to identify when an aggregate is being used to obscure rather than reveal is a core analytical skill this course is building.

Reflection 04 — What the CII cannot tell you

The videos for this section were explicit: the CII tells you where to look — not what you will find when you look there. For the pillar most relevant to your work, describe one question that a strong or weak CII score would raise — but cannot answer on its own. What additional evidence, methodology, or knowledge would you need to answer it?

Understanding the limits of an instrument is as important as understanding its capabilities. A researcher or policymaker who over-reads CII scores will make worse decisions than one who understands precisely what the instrument can and cannot do. This reflection builds that analytical discipline.

Reflection 05 — Your city's most important pillar story

Before you work with real CII data in Section 5: based on what you know about your city or research context, which pillar do you expect to be the most revealing — and why? What is the story you expect to find, and what would surprise you?

This is a prediction exercise. Making explicit predictions before working with data is one of the most effective ways to ensure that the data changes your thinking rather than confirming it. Write your hypothesis now, before you see the numbers — so that you can test it honestly in Section 5.
