From insight to intervention — synthesising your learning

Section 6 asked you to cross the hardest threshold in this course: from diagnosis to design. These five reflections test whether that crossing has happened — and prepare you for Section 7, where you apply the intervention logic across all six pillars.

These reflections are harder than previous sections — because the work is harder. You now have a diagnosis, a structural gap, an attribution question, and an intervention design. These prompts ask you to be honest about what is strong, what is missing, and what the most important political or institutional challenge is.

Reflection 01 — The structural vs surface test

Look at the first move you designed in the intervention canvas. Apply the strictest version of the structural test: does it change an institutional architecture, a feedback loop, an accountability structure, or an access pathway? Or does it improve the quality of something that is already happening?

The most common failure mode in intervention design is confusing improvement with structural change. A better-run working group is not structural. A redesigned accountability framework is. A new communications campaign is not structural. Redesigning the pathway between an existing investment and the populations not currently reached is. The distinction is not always obvious — this reflection asks you to test it rigorously.

Policymaker

Think about what happens to this intervention when the political or administrative leadership changes. Does the structural change persist — or does it depend on a specific person's commitment?

Researcher

Think about how you would operationalise "structural change" as a variable. What would distinguish this intervention from a surface improvement in your research design?

Reflection 02 — The policy trap you are most vulnerable to

Of the five policy traps — scaling too fast, optimising for visibility, ignoring system delays, the single-pillar fix, no feedback mechanism — which one is your intervention most vulnerable to in your specific institutional or political context? What is the structural reason it is a risk for you?

The five policy traps are not equally distributed — they are shaped by institutional culture, political cycle, and the nature of the problem. A researcher's intervention is most likely to fall into the no-feedback-mechanism trap. A policymaker's is most likely to fall into visibility optimisation or scaling too fast. Naming the specific trap — and the structural reason it applies in your context — is the first step to designing against it.

Policymaker

Which trap is most rewarded by your institution's current incentive structure? The trap that is institutionally rewarded is the hardest to design against.

Researcher

Which trap is most embedded in your field's research culture or funding structure? The trap that is systematically incentivised is the one most likely to appear in published research.

Reflection 03 — The spillover you did not anticipate

Working through the intervention design canvas, was there a spillover effect — positive or negative — that you had not considered before? What does that unanticipated connection reveal about the system your intervention is entering?

The most important thing about spillover effects is not the ones you plan for — it is the ones you discover in the process of designing carefully. An unanticipated positive spillover reveals a structural connection that can be deliberately amplified. An unanticipated negative spillover reveals a structural dependency that your intervention must account for. Both are diagnostically valuable.

Policymaker

Which department or institution will be most affected by the spillover you did not initially anticipate? Do they know — and do they need to be part of the design?

Researcher

How would you measure the unanticipated spillover? Is it currently tracked in any indicator set — or is it an outcome that would require new measurement design?

Reflection 04 — The time horizon your institution will tolerate

Structural interventions take time. Based on what you know about your institutional or research context: what is the longest time horizon your institution will tolerate before expecting results? And how does that compare to the realistic time horizon for the structural change your intervention is designed to produce?

The gap between institutional patience and system change timelines is one of the most structurally important determinants of intervention success. It is not a political failing — it is a systemic mismatch that must be designed around, not ignored. Naming the gap explicitly, before the intervention begins, is the precondition for managing it.

Policymaker

What is the minimum visible result you could produce within the institution's tolerance window that would protect the structural intervention long enough for it to work?

Researcher

What proxy indicator — visible in the short run — could serve as an early signal of the long-run structural change you are measuring?

Reflection 05 — The argument you need to make

Who in your institution or field is most likely to resist your intervention — not because it is wrong, but because it is structural? What argument would most effectively reach them? Not the argument that is most correct — the argument that is most likely to change their position.

This is the question that separates good intervention design from good intervention implementation. The most structurally correct intervention in the world produces nothing if it cannot navigate the political and institutional reality of the context it enters. Naming the resistance and designing the argument is part of the intervention, not a distraction from it.

Policymaker

What is the long-term cost case for the structural intervention, expressed in terms that the resisting actor values? Cost of inaction, expressed in their preferred metric, is often the most persuasive argument.

Researcher

What existing evidence base most directly supports the structural argument — and in which journal or institutional channel would it be most credible to the resisting audience?
