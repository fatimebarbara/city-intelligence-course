Why cities fail despite good data

A mini-essay, visual framework, and diagnostic scenarios — for policymakers and researchers working at the edge of data and decision-making.

Mini-essay

The paradox

Cities today are more measured than ever. We track mobility, pollution, health, housing. We build dashboards, indicators, reports. And yet, despite this data abundance, many cities are not becoming healthier, more resilient, or more inclusive. This is not a failure of measurement. It is a failure of translation — the system's inability to turn knowledge into action.

"The issue is not the absence of knowledge. It is the system's ability to turn that knowledge into action."

The broken chain

We assume a simple chain: data leads to decisions, and decisions lead to impact. In practice, this chain breaks at multiple points. Data exists — but it is fragmented across systems. Insights are produced — but they are not translated into policy action. Decisions are made — but they are not aligned with long-term outcomes.

What this creates is subtle but critical: the illusion of intelligence. A system that looks informed but does not act intelligently.

The chain breaks because data is fragmented, insights are not translated, and decisions respond to short-term pressures rather than long-term outcomes.

What this means for you

If you are a policymaker, you may recognise this as the gap between analysis and implementation. If you are a researcher, you may recognise it as the gap between evidence and impact. In both cases, the capability you are building in this course is the same: the ability to recognise when a system is data-rich but intelligence-poor — and to do something about it.

Visual framework — the five failure modes

These are not isolated problems. They are structural patterns that appear across cities, institutions, and policy areas. Recognising them is the first step to building real city intelligence.

Failure mode 01

Fragmentation

Data exists but is spread across departments, sectors, and systems that do not communicate with each other. The city is measured in silos.

Structural

Failure mode 02

Over-measurement

Too many indicators, too many dashboards — but no clear prioritisation of what actually matters for decisions and outcomes.

Cognitive

Failure mode 03

Translation gap

Insights are produced — often at high quality — but they are not translated into decisions or policy action. Evidence and implementation remain separate worlds.

Institutional

Failure mode 04

Misaligned incentives

Decisions are shaped by political cycles, institutional constraints, or short-term pressures rather than long-term urban outcomes.

Political

Failure mode 05

Short-termism

Systems react to immediate pressures instead of building long-term resilience. The most costly failures are those that are invisible in the short run — and catastrophic in the long one.

Temporal
Diagnose the failure mode

Read each scenario. Identify which failure mode is most likely at work. There may be more than one — but choose the dominant pattern.

Scenario A

A mid-sized European city commissions a comprehensive health report every two years. The reports are rigorous and widely cited in academic circles. City council meetings rarely reference them. The department head who receives them has no mandate to act on findings.

Scenario B

A national government sets up 14 separate urban monitoring systems over a decade — each managed by a different ministry, each using different definitions of "city," different time intervals, and incompatible data formats. A cross-ministry report has never been produced.

Scenario C

Six months before an election, a mayor announces a high-profile urban renewal project in a visible central district. The city's own planning data indicates that the highest infrastructure deficit — and the greatest long-term risk — is in peripheral neighbourhoods with lower media coverage.

Synthesis reflection

Reflection 01

Think of a decision in your city or institution that did not produce the expected outcome, despite good data being available. Which of the five failure modes was most present?

Be specific: name the decision, the data that existed, and the point at which the chain broke.

Reflection 02

In your current role, at which stage of the data-to-impact chain do you have the most influence? And where does your influence end?

Researchers: consider where your evidence lands and who receives it. Policymakers: consider which insights reach you and in what form.

Reflection 03

Of the five failure modes, which one is most underestimated in your city or field? Why does it stay invisible?

Is it invisible because it is structural, political, or because we have normalised it? What would it take to make it visible?
