Where is your mindset — right now?

An honest audit of the three character demands and three practices of city intelligence leadership. Not where you aspire to be. Where you actually are — and what the gap between those two tells you about the work ahead.

This audit is not a performance evaluation. It is a diagnostic — the same kind of structural reading you have applied to cities throughout this course, now applied to yourself. The most useful answers are the uncomfortable ones.

Part 1 — The three character demands

Demand 01

Intellectual honesty

Saying what the diagnostic analysis shows — in the room where something will happen as a result. Not in a seminar. Not in a paper. In the meeting where the people who made the investment are present.

PolicymakerThink of a specific recent meeting or decision. Did you present the diagnostic finding fully — including the parts that complicated the preferred narrative? Or did you soften, sequence, or omit the uncomfortable finding to manage the room?
ResearcherThink of a specific paper, grant application, or presentation. Did you present the findings that challenged the dominant framework in your field as clearly as the findings that confirmed it? Or did you contextualise the disconfirming findings in ways that made them easier to absorb?

Demand 02

Patience with complexity

Holding the structural analysis when the institution is pressing for simplification. Giving the institution what it needs rather than what it is asking for — when those two things are different.

PolicymakerThink of the last time you were asked to reduce a complex diagnostic to three priorities or one headline recommendation. Did you hold the dependency structure — or did you produce the simplification? And if you produced it, was that a genuine judgment that simplification was appropriate, or a concession to institutional pressure?
ResearcherThink of the last time a funder, editor, or conference audience asked you to simplify your findings into a clear policy implication. Did you hold the analytical precision — or did you produce a simplification that the evidence did not fully support?

Demand 03

Continuity of commitment

Staying in the work long enough for the compounding to happen. Not as discipline — as a clear answer to the question: what is the city, or the communities in it, that I am doing this for?

PolicymakerName the specific population or community whose structural situation motivates your commitment to city intelligence work. Not a category — a specific group of people whose circumstances you can describe precisely. That specificity is what makes the commitment durable.
ResearcherName the specific structural question that motivates your research commitment. Not a field — a question precise enough that you can imagine what it would mean to answer it, and what would change in the world if you did.

Part 2 — The three practices

Practice 01

The habit of the structural question

Before asking "what should we do?" — asking "what structural mechanism is producing this?" Every time. In every context. Including the ones where the structural question will slow things down.

PolicymakerIn your last five policy discussions, how many times did the conversation start from the structural mechanism rather than the proposed solution? If the answer is "rarely," that is the practice gap — not a knowledge gap. You have the framework. The practice is applying it reflexively, before the solution discussion begins.
ResearcherIn your last five research conversations — with colleagues, students, or funders — how many times did you ask "what structural mechanism is producing this pattern?" before moving to methodology? The structural question is not a hypothesis — it is a prior that shapes what method is even appropriate.

Practice 02

Reading against the metric

In any reporting context: asking what this metric is not showing. What structural outcome the organisation is not measuring. Whether that absence is intentional. This is the practice that protects against institutional self-deception.

PolicymakerName the reporting metric in your organisation that is most likely to be a substituted metric — one that measures output while a structural outcome goes unmeasured. Reading against that metric means naming it explicitly in the next performance review where it appears.
ResearcherName the primary outcome metric in your field that is most likely to be a substituted metric — one that measures what is easy to measure rather than what the intervention was designed to produce. Reading against that metric means designing research that measures both.

Practice 03

The personal feedback loop

At the end of every major cycle: asking what the diagnostic frame missed, what the intervention produced that was not predicted, and what you would design differently. This is what makes a practitioner genuinely more accurate over time — not more confident, more accurate.

PolicymakerThink of the most significant intervention or strategy you have been part of in the last three years. Apply the three questions now. What did the diagnostic frame miss? What did the intervention produce that you did not predict? What would you design differently?
ResearcherThink of the most significant study or research programme you have been part of in the last three years. Apply the three questions now. What did the theoretical frame miss? What did the data show that you did not predict? What would you design differently?
