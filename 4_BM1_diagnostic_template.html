City diagnostic template

A structured five-step diagnostic process — from reading your city's profile to identifying the binding constraint and formulating the attribution question. Select your role to see the template calibrated to your perspective.

This template is designed to be used with real CII data — either from the comparison group or from your own city's data if available. If you don't yet have specific scores, use estimated ranges based on what you know about your context. The process matters more than the precision of the numbers at this stage.

Step 01 — Context

Name your diagnostic subject

Before reading any data, name what you are diagnosing and what you are trying to understand. A diagnosis without a question is just description. The question you bring to the data shapes what you will see in it.

Policymaker

Think about a specific decision, programme, or policy area you are currently responsible for — or wish to improve. The more specific your context, the more useful the diagnosis.

Researcher

Think about the city, urban system, or policy area you are currently studying. Name the research question that makes this diagnosis relevant to your work.

The outcome that, if it doesn't improve, means this work has failed.

Step 02 — Profile

Build or estimate your city's pillar profile

Enter scores or estimates for each pillar. If you have real CII data, use it. If not, use your knowledge of your city to estimate relative performance on each dimension — the diagnostic logic works with informed estimates.

Policymaker

You don't need precise scores — a relative assessment (strong / moderate / weak) converted to a 0–1 range is enough to begin the diagnostic logic. Strong ≈ 0.70–0.85, Moderate ≈ 0.50–0.65, Weak ≈ 0.30–0.45.

Researcher

If you have access to comparable indicator data, use it to calibrate your estimates. Note which estimates are grounded in data and which are informed assumptions — this distinction will matter for your attribution analysis.

Step 03 — Outlier

Read the outlier pillar

The pillar that diverges most from the city's average is where the most important structural story lives. Never start with the composite score — start with the outlier. High outliers are strengths that may be masking gaps. Low outliers are the system's most important diagnostic signal.

Policymaker

The outlier pillar is not necessarily where you should act first — it is where you should look first. A low Healthy Society score in a city with strong economic pillars is a structural story, not a health sector problem.

Researcher

The outlier pillar is your entry point for the attribution analysis. The question is not only what the outlier score is — but why the system is producing that divergence from the expected pattern.
Based on your profile: adjust the sliders in Step 2 to see your outlier analysis.

Name the outlier and describe what pattern it reveals. Is this a structural gap, a historical underinvestment, or a systemic disconnect?

Step 04 — Structural gap

Detect the broken connection

A structural gap is not a weak score — it is a missing connection between pillars that should be performing in alignment. Identifying the gap tells you where structural change is needed, not just where performance is low.

Policymaker

The structural gap is where policy design fails to produce outcomes because two systems that should be connected are operating in silos. Naming it precisely is the prerequisite for a structural intervention.

Researcher

The structural gap is a hypothesis about a missing institutional or systemic connection. Your research can test whether the gap exists, what produces it, and what it would take to close it.

Select the two pillars whose performance diverges most unexpectedly — the connection that should exist but doesn't.

Not in indicator terms — in institutional, political, or systemic terms. What is missing? What should be connected that isn't?

Step 05 — Attribution

Formulate the attribution question

The attribution question is what transforms a CII reading into a diagnosis. It asks: which decisions, in which domains, are most likely producing the pattern I see? It is the bridge between the index and the intervention.

Policymaker

Your attribution question should name a decision domain and a structural change — not an indicator to improve. It is the question that, if answered, would tell you exactly where to act.

Researcher

Your attribution question should be testable and comparative — it should name the mechanism you are hypothesising and the evidence that would confirm or refute it.

Complete the frame: "The [outlier pillar] gap in [city] is most likely produced by decisions in [domain] — specifically [structural mechanism]. If [structural change], the gap would [predicted impact]."

Complete the frame: "The [pillar relationship] in [city] is most likely explained by [mechanism]. I would test this by [method], looking for [evidence]. The alternative explanation is [alternative] — I would distinguish between them by [approach]."

One specific, structural, achievable action. Not a study. Not a meeting. A change.
