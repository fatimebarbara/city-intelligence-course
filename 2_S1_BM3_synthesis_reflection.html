Reflection 01 — The paradox in your world

In your city or institution, where do you see the data-impact paradox most clearly? What do you measure well — and what outcomes remain stubbornly unchanged despite good data?

This section opened with a paradox: more data does not automatically lead to better outcomes. This prompt asks you to locate that paradox in your own experience — not as a global observation, but as a specific, nameable gap in your context.

Reflection 02 — Naming the failure mode

Of the five failure modes — fragmentation, over-measurement, translation gap, misaligned incentives, short-termism — which one is most present in the situation you described above? What evidence supports that diagnosis?

Naming a failure mode is not about blame. It is about precision. The same problem can have very different solutions depending on which structural pattern is producing it. A translation gap requires different action than misaligned incentives — even when both produce the same surface symptom.

Reflection 03 — Your position in the chain

In the data-to-impact chain — data → insight → decision → action → outcome — at which stage do you sit? And at which stage do you most often see the chain break?

Understanding your position is not self-limiting — it is strategic. It tells you which relationships and interventions are within your sphere of influence, and where you need to work with others to keep the chain intact.

Reflection 04 — One decision, reread

Choose a specific decision made recently in your city or institution — one you were close to. Reread it through the lens of the five failure modes. Does the decision look different now? What would have changed if the dominant failure mode had been addressed?

This is the hardest prompt — it asks not just to observe, but to imagine. What alternative was foreclosed by the failure mode? This is not about criticism. It is about building a more precise vocabulary for what went right, what went wrong, and what is still possible.

Reflection 05 — One thing you will do differently

Based on your reflections above, name one concrete action you will take in the next 30 days. A question you will ask in a meeting, a conversation you will initiate, a dataset you will seek out, or a framing you will introduce. Make it specific and achievable.

Learning without commitment to action is information, not intelligence. This course is designed to change how you judge and act — not only how you understand. This prompt is the bridge between insight and the world you return to.

Section 3 — What city intelligence actually is

You now know how and why cities fail despite good data. Section 3 asks the harder question: what would a city need in order to succeed? It introduces the concept of city intelligence — not as a technology or a measurement system, but as an emergent property of how a city's information, governance, decisions, and outcomes are connected.
