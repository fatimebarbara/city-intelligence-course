Failure mode 01

Mandate fragmentation

No one owns the connection between steps — accountability stops at each departmental border

▾

Institutional signals this failure mode is present

Your sequence crosses departments that have no formal shared accountability mechanism — no joint reporting line, no shared budget, no cross-mandate performance review
When you describe the sequence to each department head, each one engages with their step — but no one asks about the connection to the next step
The strategy document exists, but no person has been assigned responsibility for the sequence as a whole — only for individual interventions
Departments celebrate outputs independently — Step 1 is called a success before Step 2 has even been designed
Severity in your context:

Which departments are most likely to fragment — and why? What is the institutional reason the connection between steps would break?

Failure mode 02

Metric substitution

Departments optimise for their own output metrics rather than the shared outcome — success on paper, failure in practice

▾

Institutional signals this failure mode is present

Each department in your sequence has its own performance framework with metrics that predate your strategy — and those metrics will be used to evaluate the departments regardless of your strategy's logic
There is no shared outcome metric that all departments in the sequence are measured against — each reports separately on different data
When you ask "how will we know the sequence is working?", the answers from different departments describe different outcomes that may be individually achievable without the strategy succeeding
Previous cross-departmental programmes in your context were reported as successful while their structural objectives were not met — the outputs looked fine but nothing structurally changed
Severity in your context:

What are the existing output metrics that are most likely to substitute for the shared outcome? What would "success" look like on paper if the sequence is actually failing?

Failure mode 03

Political cycle truncation

The sequence requires more time than the political cycle allows — the thread breaks at the transition

▾

Institutional signals this failure mode is present

Your compounding sequence requires more time than the current political mandate — there is likely to be a leadership transition before the compounding effect is visible in the data
The strategy's most important outcome — the thing that makes it worth doing — will arrive after the people who designed it have left office or moved on
There is no transition-proofing in the current design — no mechanism to ensure that an incoming administration inherits the sequence logic, not just the individual programmes
In your context, incoming administrations routinely rebrand or redesign programmes inherited from the previous leadership
Severity in your context:

What is the realistic time horizon of your sequence? What is the next expected transition point? What is the gap between them?

Failure mode 04

Institutional capture

The structural intervention is gradually redesigned by the most powerful actor to serve their interests rather than the structural goal

▾

Institutional signals this failure mode is present

Your sequence requires a powerful institutional actor — a large department, a dominant service provider, or a well-resourced agency — to change the way it operates in ways that may not be in its immediate interest
That actor controls access to the resources, data, or relationships your sequence depends on — giving them leverage over the design even if they are not formally in charge
In previous cross-departmental programmes, the most powerful department typically ended up managing the programme and setting its terms — even when the programme was designed to serve a different objective
The structural element of your intervention — the part that changes the architecture — is the hardest to measure and therefore the most vulnerable to being deprioritised in favour of the visible, measurable elements
Severity in your context:

Who is the most powerful institutional actor your strategy depends on — and whose interest is most misaligned with the structural change it requires?
