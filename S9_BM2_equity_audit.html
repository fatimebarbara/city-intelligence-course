Question 01

Who benefits from your city's performance on each pillar — and who does not?

PolicymakerLook at your city's strongest pillar. Who — specifically — benefits from that strength? Name a population group that does not benefit from it, and describe the structural reason they are excluded from the performance your city is achieving on that dimension.
ResearcherFor your study city: which population groups are systematically underrepresented in the data sources that feed the pillar you are analysing? The same question applies across cities in any comparative analysis. The question is not only who benefits from performance — but who is invisible in the measurement.

What disaggregated data — by district, income, age, ethnicity, or other dimension — would make the exclusion visible rather than hidden behind the aggregate?

Question 02

Are the populations excluded from your city's Foundational investments the same populations experiencing the worst Healthy Society outcomes?

PolicymakerThis question tests whether your city's Foundational and Healthy Society gaps are structurally linked — whether addressing one would address the other — or whether they are driven by different structural mechanisms requiring different interventions.
ResearcherThe research question here is whether the Foundational–Healthy Society correlation (r=0.911 at city level) holds at the sub-city level — and whether it is driven by the same populations in each city or whether the mechanism varies. This is a testable hypothesis that district-level data can address.

If the same populations carry both gaps, a single structural intervention can address both. If different populations carry different gaps, the sequence needs to be designed accordingly.

Question 03

Does the intervention you designed in Section 6 improve the average, the distribution, or both — and if only one, which one and why?

PolicymakerThis is the most politically difficult question in Section 10. An intervention that improves the average is easier to sell — it produces a headline CII score improvement. An intervention that improves the distribution may produce a smaller headline improvement while reducing structural inequality. Name which yours does, honestly.
ResearcherThis is the most methodologically important question for evaluation design. The primary outcome of your study should specify whether you are measuring average improvement, distributional change, or both — because these require different measurement frameworks and different sample designs.

Or if only the distribution: how would you make the distributional improvement visible in the aggregate metric, so it is not invisible to decision-makers who track CII scores?
