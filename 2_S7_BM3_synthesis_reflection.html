Reflection 01 — The failure mode you recognise

Of the four implementation failure modes — mandate fragmentation, metric substitution, political cycle truncation, institutional capture — which one have you personally witnessed, experienced, or caused? Name the specific situation, the mechanism through which it operated, and what the actual cost was.

This reflection is the most practically grounded in the section. The failure modes are not abstract risks — they are patterns that appear again and again in the same institutional contexts. Naming a specific experience, rather than a general agreement with the framework, is the test of whether the taxonomy is analytically useful or just conceptually agreeable.

PolicymakerThe most honest answer often involves a failure mode you contributed to — not one you observed from a distance. What decision, reporting structure, or accountability design on your part made a cross-boundary strategy harder to sustain?
ResearcherThe research answer often involves a study you designed or contributed to that operationalised substituted metrics — measuring the output rather than the structural outcome — without naming that choice explicitly.

Reflection 02 — Why good will is not enough

Section 8 argues that implementation failure modes are structural, not motivational. They do not require bad actors, poor intentions, or inadequate evidence. They are the predictable outputs of institutional architectures that were not designed for cross-boundary strategies. Do you agree — and what does that mean for how you would normally try to address them?

This is the conceptual crux of Section 8. If you believe failure modes are motivational — produced by unwillingness, ignorance, or political opposition — the remedies are communication, training, and advocacy. If you believe they are structural — produced by incentive misalignment, mandate design, and accountability architecture — the remedies are governance changes. The distinction determines the entire design logic of Section 8.

PolicymakerThink about a past experience where better communication, stronger political will, or more evidence did not prevent a strategy from failing in implementation. What structural feature was producing the failure that these approaches could not address?
ResearcherConsider the implementation failure literature in your field. How does it apportion causation between motivational and structural explanations? Does the Section 8 framework align with or challenge the dominant account?

Reflection 03 — The hardest failure mode to name in advance

Of the four failure modes, which is the hardest to name before it has already broken the chain — and why? What makes it difficult to detect early, and what would a genuinely useful early-warning indicator look like?

The asymmetry between failure modes matters for governance design. Some are detectable early (mandate fragmentation is often visible in the first joint meeting). Others compound silently for months before the signal appears (institutional capture often looks like programme improvement until the structural element has already been removed). Knowing which failure modes are hardest to detect early shapes which governance countermeasures need to be built before the strategy launches, rather than after the signal appears.

PolicymakerIn your institutional context, which failure mode is most likely to be invisible to you as the strategy owner — because it operates in the administrative or reporting layer rather than in the political or strategic layer you typically inhabit?
ResearcherWhich failure mode is most likely to be invisible in the data? If you were evaluating a strategy that had been subject to this failure mode, what would the data show — and what would it not show?

Reflection 04 — The failure mode your institution produces most reliably

Every institutional context has a dominant failure mode — the pattern it produces most reliably, for structural reasons specific to how it is organised. Which failure mode does your institution or research context produce most reliably? What feature of the institutional architecture produces it?

This is the most uncomfortable question in Section 8 — because it asks you to name a structural feature of an institution you are part of as a source of predictable failure. Not a bad actor. Not an unlucky circumstance. A feature of the architecture that will produce the same failure mode again and again unless it is structurally changed.

PolicymakerThe structural feature is usually visible in how performance is measured and rewarded. Which failure mode does your institution's current performance architecture make most likely — and is anyone currently responsible for changing that architecture?
ResearcherThe structural feature in research contexts is often in how funding is allocated and evaluated. Which failure mode does your field's current funding and evaluation architecture make most likely for cross-disciplinary or cross-institutional work?

Reflection 05 — The governance question Section 8 must answer for you

Section 8 will ask you to design the governance architecture that protects your strategy. Based on your threat mapping and resistance audit: what is the single most important governance question Section 8 must answer for you? Not a general governance question — the specific structural design challenge that your sequence, in your context, most urgently needs a countermeasure for.

This is the integration reflection. If you can name your governance question precisely — with the specific failure mode, the specific institutional mechanism, and the specific structural gap — Section 8's tools will be immediately applicable to your context. If the question is still general, Section 8 will feel like theory. The specificity of the question determines the practical value of the answer.

PolicymakerFrame the question as a design brief: "How do I design [specific governance mechanism] to protect [specific step in the sequence] from [specific failure mode], given [specific institutional constraint]?"
ResearcherFrame the question as a research hypothesis: "Cities with [specific governance feature] will show lower rates of [specific failure mode] in cross-boundary strategies — testable by [method] using [data]."
